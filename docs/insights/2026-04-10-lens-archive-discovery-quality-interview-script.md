# Lens Archive Discovery Quality Interview Script

- Status: Draft
- Topic: lens-archive-discovery-quality
- Probe: P1 Structured creator interviews
- Goal: Test whether repeatable discovery quality is important enough to change creator behavior, whether "right viewers" can be defined clearly, and whether the current works-first path feels credible.

## 1. Interview Guardrails

- Do not pitch the product in detail.
- Do not ask "would you use this?" before the participant has described their current workflow.
- Force comparison against the participant's current stack: Instagram, Behance, personal site, referrals, DMs, niche groups, or anything else they already use.
- Ask for recent concrete examples, not generic opinions.
- If the participant says they want "more exposure," always ask "from whom, for what outcome?"

## 2. Recruiting Criteria

- Publishes photography work at least once per month.
- Uses 2 or more channels for visibility or client/collaboration discovery.
- Has at least one real goal tied to discovery this year:
  - paid work
  - better collaborators
  - stronger audience
  - peer critique
  - long-term reputation
- Exclude participants who only want vanity growth and cannot name any downstream outcome.

## 4. Interview Flow

### Part A: Current Workflow

1. Walk me through where you currently publish your work and why each channel exists.
2. Which channel do you treat as your real home, if any?
3. Which channel do you trust most for serious discovery, and which do you use only because you feel you have to?

### Part B: Last Valuable Discovery Event

4. Tell me about the last time someone valuable discovered your work.
5. Who found you?
6. How did they find you?
7. What happened next?
8. Was that outcome repeatable, or did it feel like luck?

### Part C: Define "Right Viewers"

9. When you say you want the right people to see your work, who are those people this year?
10. Give me your top 3 viewer types and rank them.
11. For each one, what makes them more valuable than generic engagement?
12. If you got more likes but fewer of these people, would that still feel like success?

### Part D: Repeatability and Behavior Change

13. How often do you need this kind of discovery outcome for it to matter: weekly, monthly, around launches, or only occasionally?
14. If a product could reliably get your work in front of those people, what would you do differently?
15. What would you stop doing, reduce, or deprioritize?
16. What would make you refuse to trust that promise?

### Part E: Substitute Pressure

17. If you had to keep only your current stack, what still works well enough today?
18. Where does your current stack break down?
19. What would a new discovery surface have to do better than Behance, Instagram, Glass, personal sites, or referrals to be worth attention?

### Part F: Credibility of the Current Loop

20. Imagine a works-first product where someone discovers a photo, clicks into the creator profile, then decides whether to follow or message. Does that path feel credible or weak to you?
21. At what point in that path does trust usually break?
22. What would make follow or message feel useful rather than spammy?

### Part G: Closing

23. If we completely removed the promise of generic reach and only promised repeatable discovery by the right viewers, would that still be interesting?
24. What is the harshest reason this idea would fail for you personally?

## 5. Scoring Rubric

Score each dimension from 0 to 2.

- Right-viewer clarity
  - 0: cannot define any distinct viewer type
  - 1: can name viewer types, but they are vague or overlapping
  - 2: can define specific viewer types with clear downstream value

- Repeatability need
  - 0: discovery only matters episodically or as luck
  - 1: repeatability matters, but only loosely
  - 2: repeatable discovery is a clear, ongoing need

- Behavior-change willingness
  - 0: would not change publishing behavior
  - 1: might experiment, but without meaningful effort shift
  - 2: would clearly reallocate time, channel priority, or content effort

- Trust in product-mediated discovery
  - 0: actively distrusts the idea
  - 1: skeptical but open
  - 2: believes it could be credible under clear conditions

## 6. Decision Rules

- Strong pass signal:
  - At least 6 of 10 participants score 2 on right-viewer clarity or repeatability need.
  - At least 5 of 10 participants score 2 on behavior-change willingness.

- Warning signal:
  - Participants want generic reach more than qualified discovery.
  - Participants can describe desired audiences, but not in a way that changes product direction.

- Kill signal:
  - 2 or fewer participants can define right viewers clearly.
  - Nobody would materially reallocate effort even if the promise were true.
  - Most participants say their existing stack is already good enough.

## 7. Synthesis Questions

- What viewer types appeared repeatedly across interviews?
- Which downstream outcomes mattered most: work, collaboration, critique, reputation, or something else?
- Did people want ongoing discovery, or only occasional launch moments?
- What broke trust most often: randomness, spam, poor fit, or platform fatigue?
- Does the evidence support a narrow first segment, or does it show the thesis is too broad?
